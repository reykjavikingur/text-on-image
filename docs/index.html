Text on Images

The decision to put text on images can make a design more visually appealing, but when the decision is part of a design system that must accommodate a large variety of possible combinations of text and image, you must take certain concerns into account and incorporate them into the design system as a whole.

What we see

Suppose you are mocking up examples for a design system, and you are given 10 combinations of text and images to display in an overlapping manner. But in some cases, the text becomes illegible where it overlaps parts of the associated image that are not in high enough contrast with the text color.

What we often do

As a designer you have many possible workarounds at your disposal, and it is common to treat each combination differently according to its respective circumstances. For instance, you can:

  • Change the color of the text
  • Insert line breaks and indentations at strategic positions in the text
  • Shift the entire text field's position within the image
  • Dim or brighten parts of an image where text contrast appears too low

Why it fails

If your design system will become part of a CMS, then it will need to render potentially thousands of combinations of text and images, most of which you may never see while working on a few dozen hypothetical examples. The people responsible for writing the text, taking the photos, compiling the digital feeds, and managing the layouts will not necessarily have your advantage of visualizing the final result. They will not necessarily be able to visit every combination of text and image and apply some equivalent of the workarounds that you used when creating the mock examples.

A design system for rendering managed content must work properly for all possible combinations of content without the need to inspect and hand-customize each one.

An example of what not to do

Simply switching the text color from black to white improves legibility significantly, but that only works because this image happens to be dark enough. You can easily imagine a different image where white text would also fail legibility. Even developers are quick to resort to hand-customizations, for instance, introducing some data-driven mechanism that lets a content manager select a text color. But that only moves the problem one step further away. There will always be some possible image in the future with a combination of pixels that will not provide enough contrast throughout the text field, no matter what color you use. The same principle applies to changing text positions or inserting line breaks, etc. That whole approach is wrong, because the problem is not the fault of the particular choice of text and image.

Stop blaming the data. The fault lies with the system itself.

Solution #1: Text background strip

An easy, low-fidelity solution is to set the background-color CSS property of the text element. This example uses a semi-opaque text background color.

Solution #2: Wash out the image

If your implementation uses the background-image CSS property, you can add a linear-gradient transform to increase contrast for whatever text color you use.

Solution #3: Text Shadow

My personal favorite solution is the text-shadow CSS property, where you can control the color, offset, and blur radius. Simply by wrapping the text in a faint glow, you can significantly improve legibility with minimal visual effect.
