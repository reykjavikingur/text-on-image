Text on Images

The decision to put text on images can make a design more visually appealing, but when the decision is part of a design system that must accommodate a large variety of possible combinations of text and image, you must take certain concerns into account and incorporate them into the design system as a whole.

What we see

Suppose you are mocking up examples for a design system, and you are given 10 combinations of text and images to display in an overlapping manner. But in some cases, the text becomes illegible where it overlaps parts of the associated image that are not in high enough contrast with the text color.

What we often do

As a designer or front-end developer, you have many possible workarounds at your disposal, and it is common to treat each combination differently according to its respective circumstances. For instance, you can:

  • Change the color of the text
  • Insert line breaks and indentations at strategic positions in the text
  • Shift the entire text field's position within the image
  • Dim or brighten parts of an image where text contrast appears too low

Why it fails

If your design system will become part of a CMS, then it will need to render potentially thousands of combinations of text and images, most of which you may never see while working on a few dozen hypothetical examples. The people responsible for writing the text, taking the photos, compiling the digital feeds, and managing the layouts will not necessarily have your advantage of visualizing the final result. They will not necessarily be able to visit every combination of text and image and apply some equivalent of the workarounds that you used when creating the mock examples.

A design system for rendering managed content must work properly for all possible combinations of content without the need to inspect and hand-customize each one.

This image is an extreme case but perfectly realistic. Circumstantial fixes such as switching text color are not universally feasible. Any hand-customizations that you must resort to within your design system transfers the same burden to content managers, who have to deal with much more data and don't necessarily have the design or development tools to facilitate these workarounds.

Don't blame the data for faults that lie with the design system itself.

Text Shadow

The text-shadow CSS property lets you control the color, offset, and blur radius. Simply by wrapping the text in a faint glow, you can significantly improve legibility with minimal visual effect.
